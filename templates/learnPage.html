Password Strength Checker

What Makes a Strong Password?

Great question! A strong password is defined by one key characteristic: randomness! Then, why do all the websites outline the following requirements for a password: 1 uppercase letter, 1 lowercase letter, 1 special character, 1 number, and a minimum length of 8 characters. Many believe that this makes for a stronger password because it’s more “complex” and is hard for a human to guess – but, this complexity actually makes it easier for a computer or a program to crack your password.

In fact, it is impossible for the human brain to choose a password that is truly random. When we try to identify a password that is both “complex” and easy for us to remember, we often end up following a prescribed pattern that is very easy for the computer to guess. As the XKCD comic suggests, a password such as “correct horse battery staple” chosen at random is stronger than a password that follows all the so-called password requirements to a T. To the human mind, “Tr0ub4dor&3” will look like the stronger password. However, it is actually extremely easy for a computer to guess. This apparent contradiction can be further understood through the concept of entropy.

What is Entropy?

Entropy is a measure of the randomness of a password (i.e., how many different possibilities an attacker must consider to crack your password). Length is one of the primary factors defining entropy; with more length, there’s more room for randomness. Entropy can also be increased by adding more characters to the mix – i.e., lowercase letters, uppercase letters, numbers, and symbols. But, not the way a common sign up form suggests – to be truly strong, the password must be >AS RANDOM AS POSSIBLE. The more we try to build a structure/pattern/uniformness to the random password generation, the weaker our password becomes in the face of password crackers and other tools. That said, a truly random password such as “5&2CcKMvD8vy” is not something that the average internet user can remember, recollect, and use. As a result, we are left with two seemingly contrasting ideas: guessability and memorability.

Balancing Guessability & Memorability

The average American internet user is projected to have 300 accounts protected by passwords by 2022 – an impossible number of passwords to remember and recollect. As such, we recommend that you focus on remembering three to four passwords that are absolutely critical (i.e., Apple ID, Gmail, and laptop password) and use a secure, trusted password manager to aid with the rest.

Now, let’s talk about how to create strong and memorable passwords for those three to four critical accounts that you will have to memorize. The best password is one that is hard for an attacker to crack but easy for a user to remember. Passwords that we remember and recollect easily end up connected to us in some way; for instance, your mother’s maiden name may be quite obscure, but it may also be one of the first things any decent hacker may try. The strength of a password lies in its randomness, but even the strongest password can fail if you (1) cannot remember and recollect it as necessary or (2) rely on poor password usage strategies. This includes everything from writing it down on a sticky note, reusing the same strong password in multiple places, and saving passwords in unsecure locations. Theoretically, a password “5&2CcKMvD8vy” is strong, and a password “2y5!*iq8e!g#jlgR2enywnj14GOak6@*” is stronger, but neither is intuitive or memorable. So, how can we create a password that is both strong and memorable?

Our answer is Diceware. Diceware is a strategy to choose passwords based on dice rolls. The strength of this strategy relies on the true randomness generated through several independent dice rolls. Under this strategy, you must roll an ordinary dice five times and use the resulting sequence of values to choose a word from the Diceware list. The original Diceware list is comprised of 7776 short words, easy to remember abbreviations, and special characters. Here’s a short excerpt of a Diceware list:

16655 clause
16656 claw
16661 clay
16662 clean
16663 clear

The strategy recommends a password with a minimum length of 16 to 32 characters (approximately, 4-5 words as each word in the list is an average of 4.2 letters long). As such, a user may re-roll their dice 5 times to select an additional three to four words to add to their password. The resulting password may resemble the following: correct horse battery staple. While these are common dictionary words, when generated randomly, the resulting entropy is 164.7, which makes up quite the strong password. More importantly, in contrast to a password like “2y5!*iq8e!g#jlgR2enywnj14GOak6@*”, “correct horse battery staple” is much more memorable. You will have to put some time into memorizing it, but once memorized this will serve you much better than your mother’s maiden name, “complex” password like @ppl3s!$!, and is definitely more memorable than “5&2CcKMvD8vy” in the future.

How Does Our Password Checker Work?

Our password checker operates by checking to see if an entered password satisfies the following requirements:

  • The password is at least 12 characters long
  • The password contains at least one capital letter, symbol, and number
  • The password contains a sufficient number of unique characters
  • The password does not contain a common dictionary word
  • The password does not contain a reversed common dictionary word

This allows us to follow the requirements of most modern websites and also protect passwords against dictionary attacks. A dictionary attack is when an attacker conducts a brute force run through common words and phrases to decode a password. Passwords must be invulnerable to dictionary attacks.

After each check is completed, our strength checker will return all vulnerabilities in the password to the screen.

How Does Our Password Generator Work?

Through our password generator, you can generate a password that is either 14 to 20 characters, 20 to 28 characters, or 28 to 32 characters long. Our password generator is based on Diceware, a strategy to choose passwords through several dice rolls. We use python’s in-built random() function to simulate 4 six-sided dice rolls and match the corresponding values to the Electronic Frontier Foundation’s (EFF) wordlist. This wordlist is a modified version of the original Diceware list; it is made up of shorter words and excludes special characters and easy-to-remember abbreviations. We adopted the modified wordlist from the EFF because the original Diceware list included special characters and abbreviations that added unnecessary complexity to our password generation strategy. Each word randomly chosen from this wordlist yields an additional 10.3 bits of entropy. Selecting four to five words from this list based on the user’s chosen length serves as the base for our password generation strategy. Given that most modern websites require the inclusion of numbers, symbols, and also capital letters in passwords, we inserted all of the mentioned into each word at the same random index. While the original Diceware strategy discusses a different way for adding special characters or numbers, we decided on this way because it allows for more memorable passwords while still retaining some degree of randomness.

Upon selecting a length and clicking generate, a user will see the following populate their screen: a generated password, the original randomized word list, the position of the inserted symbol, and the position of the capitalized letters. This is all provided back to the user to ensure that the user (1) understands how the password was generated and (2) can better remember and recollect their password.

How Does Our Password Entropy Calculator Work

Through our password entropy calculator, you can check the entropy of your password. Entropy is a measure of the randomness of a password (i.e., how many different possibilities an attacker must consider to crack your password). Our entropy calculator calculates entropy through the following formula: Entropy (E) = L x log2(R).

L represents the length of the password, and R represents the size of the pool of characters. Entropy-wise, you can make your password stronger by either (1) increasing the length or (2) expanding the character set (i.e., adding uppercase letters, digits, symbols). However, a longer password with only lowercase characters can be far stronger than a short and complex password; this is also obvious from the mathematical formula.

Our entropy calculator allows users to enter a password, and outputs the resulting entropy value. To make sense of your value, please know how your password’s entropy compares in the following chart:

Entropy (in bits) Strength
25-30 bits weak
60-80 bits average
~100 bits strong
>100 bits very strong (recommended)

We recommend that you prioritize length to increase the strength of your password; it is much more effective than increasing your character set by 10. That said, it is important to note that a high entropy value alone does not guarantee a strong password. We provide an entropy calculator to help you evaluate the strength of the password you randomly generated using your own Diceware or our password generator. Entropy is a measure of randomness, and our entropy calculator assumes that your password was generated randomly. If you use a password that is relevant to you personally, reuse a password, or use a leaked password, your password’s actual entropy value is likely much, much lower. Please be cautious as you use our entropy calculator to help you generate strong, secure passwords.

Summary

  1. DO NOT: reuse passwords or use passwords that are tied to you personally regardless of how obscure they are.
  2. DO: use password managers to store the majority of your day-to-day passwords, and memorize the few crucial ones that you will need (Gmail, Apple ID, laptop password).
  3. DO NOT: use common dictionary words as password unless they were generated randomly.
  4. DO: generate your password randomly; randomness is key to a strong password. Diceware is one of the most prominent strategies to truly random passwords that are also memorable. Feel free to try our generator that uses the same strategy!
  5. DO NOT: rely primarily on our entropy checker to verify your password’s strength; please know that the true measure of strength lies with randomness.
  6. DO: increase the size of your password to increase their strength; once again, be mindful that this strategy only works if you avoid common dictionary words and use random password generation.
